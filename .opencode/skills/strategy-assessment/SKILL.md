# strategy-assessment

## 1. Skill name
Strategy Assessment

## 2. One-line purpose
Evaluate and formulate strategy by diagnosing the situation, defining a guiding policy, and designing coherent actions that address the core challenge.

## 3. When to use this skill
- When defining a product or company strategy
- When reviewing an existing strategy that is not producing results
- When deciding between competing initiatives or directions
- When the team is spread too thin and needs focus
- Before making a major investment or pivot

## 4. When not to use this skill
- For routine operational decisions that don't require strategic analysis
- When the situation is well-understood and the current strategy is working
- For purely tactical or technical decisions without strategic implications

## 5. Core principles
- **Strategy is not a goal. It is a response to a challenge**: "We want to grow 50%" is not a strategy. A strategy starts with a diagnosis of the challenge and designs a way to overcome it (GSBS Ch.1).
- **The kernel of strategy has three parts**: A diagnosis (defines the nature of the challenge), a guiding policy (the overall approach to overcoming it), and coherent actions (coordinated steps that make the policy real) (GSBS Ch.1).
- **Bad strategy is more common than good strategy**: Bad strategy is not the absence of strategy; it is a specific kind of thinking: fluff (vague words that sound good), failure to face the problem (ignoring the hard challenge), mistaking goals for strategy (listing objectives instead of designing a response), and bad strategic objectives (objectives that are infeasible or irrelevant) (GSBS Ch.2-5).
- **A good strategy identifies the key challenge and focuses resources on it**: You cannot do everything. Strategy is about what NOT to do (GSBS Ch.1, Ch.6).
- **Leverage and focus**: Use the most critical points of leverage to amplify effort. Concentrate power at the decisive point (GSBS Ch.6).
- **Proximate objectives**: Make the objective close enough to be achievable but far enough to matter. Vague, distant objectives produce no coherent action (GSBS Ch.7).
- **Think in chains of causality**: Before committing to a strategy, trace the causal chain: "If we do X, then Y happens, which leads to Z." If the chain is broken at any point, the strategy will fail (GSBS Ch.1).
- **Strategy must account for the competition**: What will competitors do when you make your move? Anticipate their response (GSBS Ch.1).
- **The system creates the behavior**: Before blaming people, examine the system structure. Poor outcomes are usually caused by system design, not individual failures (TIS Ch.3).
- **Feedback loops drive behavior**: Reinforcing loops drive growth; balancing loops resist change. Identify the loops that govern your situation (TIS Ch.1).
- **Leverage points are where small changes produce big effects**: Find the point in the system where a small intervention can shift the entire system's behavior (TIS Ch.6).
- **The build-measure-learn cycle needs a strategy to guide which experiments to run** (LS Ch.7, GSBS Ch.1).

## 6. Step-by-step method

### Step 1: Diagnose the situation
- Identify the core challenge: What is the fundamental obstacle to achieving the desired outcome? (GSBS Ch.1)
- Ask: "What is going on here? What are the patterns? What is the real problem?"
- Look for the system structure: stocks, flows, feedback loops, delays (TIS Ch.1-3)
- Map the competitive landscape: who else is trying to achieve this? What are their advantages?
- Identify constraints: resources, time, capabilities, regulations
- If the diagnosis is wrong, the strategy will fail regardless of execution (GSBS Ch.1)

### Step 2: Identify leverage points
- Where can a small effort produce a large effect? (TIS Ch.6)
- Common leverage points (from most effective to least) (TIS Ch.6):
  - The mindset / paradigm out of which the system arises
  - The goals of the system
  - The structure of information flows
  - The rules of the system (incentives, constraints, permissions)
  - The structure of stocks and flows
  - The size of buffers and stabilizing stocks
  - The length of delays (relative to the rate of change)
- Focus on the top 3 leverage points that are within your control

### Step 3: Define the guiding policy
- The guiding policy is the overall approach: not a detailed plan, but a direction (GSBS Ch.1)
- It should address the diagnosis: if the diagnosis is X, the policy should be "we will overcome X by..."
- It should create focus: what will we NOT do? (GSBS Ch.6)
- It should be specific enough to guide action but general enough to allow adaptation
- Test: if someone reads the guiding policy, can they figure out what to do (and what not to do)?

### Step 4: Design coherent actions
- List the specific actions that implement the guiding policy (GSBS Ch.1)
- Actions must be coherent: they should work together toward the same objective, not pull in different directions
- Actions should concentrate resources at the critical point (GSBS Ch.6)
- Each action should have a clear owner, timeline, and success metric
- Test: is each action clearly connected to the guiding policy? If not, it's a distraction

### Step 5: Anticipate competitive responses
- What will competitors, customers, and regulators do when you execute your strategy? (GSBS Ch.1)
- What would make your strategy fail? List the top 5 failure modes
- For each failure mode, define a contingency
- If the anticipated response neutralizes your strategy, you need a different strategy

### Step 6: Build and test the strategy
- Use Build-Measure-Learn: treat the strategy as a hypothesis (LS Ch.7)
- Identify the leap-of-faith assumptions in the diagnosis, policy, and actions
- Run experiments to test the riskiest assumptions
- Iterate on the strategy based on learning
- Don't wait until the strategy is perfect; launch it, learn, and adapt

### Step 7: Review and update
- Schedule regular strategy reviews (quarterly or after major milestones)
- For each review, check: is the diagnosis still correct? Is the guiding policy still working? Do the actions need adjustment?
- When the situation changes significantly, revisit the entire strategy
- Kill strategies that are not working. Sunk cost is not a reason to continue (GSBS Ch.2)

## 7. Decision rules
- **If the strategy document is full of buzzwords (fluff), rewrite it in concrete terms** (GSBS Ch.2).
- **If the strategy does not identify a specific challenge, it is a list of goals, not a strategy** (GSBS Ch.1).
- **If the strategy does not say what you will NOT do, it lacks focus** (GSBS Ch.6).
- **If the causal chain from actions to outcomes has a broken link, the strategy will fail** (GSBS Ch.1).
- **If the strategy ignores what competitors will do, it is incomplete** (GSBS Ch.1).
- **If the strategy blames people for failures that are caused by system structure, the real problem is the system** (TIS Ch.3).
- **If the team cannot articulate the strategy in one sentence, it's too complex** (GSBS Ch.1).
- **If the strategy has not changed in a year while the market has changed significantly, it's likely outdated** (LS Ch.8).
- **If the strategy requires every team to change direction together, the transition cost may kill it. Sequence the changes** (GSBS Ch.6).
- **If a leverage point requires changing people's mindsets, it's the most powerful but the hardest. Plan for a longer time horizon** (TIS Ch.6).

## 8. Common mistakes
- Mistaking a goal (e.g., "grow revenue 20%") for a strategy (GSBS Ch.3).
- Writing strategy documents full of fluff: "customer-centric, synergistic, world-class" (GSBS Ch.2).
- Refusing to face the problem—especially if the problem is that the product is not good enough (GSBS Ch.4).
- Setting strategic objectives that are not actionable (GSBS Ch.5).
- Trying to do everything and ending up with no focus (GSBS Ch.6).
- Setting objectives that are too far in the future to guide current action (GSBS Ch.7).
- Ignoring system dynamics: pushing harder on a balancing loop without changing the structure (TIS Ch.3).
- Choosing easy-to-measure metrics over the metrics that matter (GSBS Ch.2).
- Not updating the strategy when the diagnosis changes (LS Ch.8).
- Letting a bad strategy persist because of sunk cost or ego (GSBS Ch.2).

## 10. Quality checklist
- [ ] Diagnosis identifies the specific challenge (not a symptom)
- [ ] Strategy has all three kernel elements (diagnosis, policy, actions)
- [ ] No fluff, buzzwords, or vague language
- [ ] Strategy says what we will NOT do
- [ ] Actions are coherent (work together, not pull apart)
- [ ] Competitive responses are anticipated
- [ ] Riskiest assumptions are identified and testable
- [ ] System dynamics are considered (not just linear thinking)
- [ ] Strategy is articulable in one sentence
- [ ] Review process is scheduled

## 11. Source books used
- Primary: Good Strategy Bad Strategy (Rumelt)
- Support: Thinking in Systems (Meadows), The Lean Startup (Ries), Inspired (Cagan)

## 12. Notes on how the books complement each other
GSBS provides the core strategy framework: the kernel (diagnosis, guiding policy, coherent actions), the sources of bad strategy, and the principles of leverage, focus, and proximate objectives. TIS adds the systems thinking lens: stocks and flows, feedback loops, delays, and leverage points—revealing the deeper structure behind strategic challenges. LS adds the learning methodology: treating strategy as a hypothesis, running experiments, and iterating. Inspired adds the product management perspective: aligning product strategy with business strategy, and the relationship between vision, strategy, and roadmap. Together they form a complete strategy methodology: from diagnosis through system analysis to execution and iterative learning.
