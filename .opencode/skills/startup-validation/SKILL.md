# startup-validation

## 1. Skill name
Startup Validation

## 2. One-line purpose
Validate a business idea by talking to customers, defining testable hypotheses, and running experiments before building a product.

## 3. When to use this skill
- Before writing any code for a new product or feature
- When you have an idea but are not sure if anyone will pay for it
- When the team disagrees on what to build
- When you need to decide whether to pivot or persevere
- Before raising funding or hiring a team

## 4. When not to use this skill
- When the product is already validated and you are iterating on known market fit
- For infrastructure or internal tools where the customer is yourself
- When speed of execution matters more than validation (known emergency)

## 5. Core principles
- **Your opinion doesn't matter, the market's opinion does**: Every idea is unvalidated until proven otherwise. The goal is to learn, not to confirm your biases (TMT Ch.1).
- **Don't ask what people want, ask about their life**: "The Mom Test" means you can't trust compliments. Instead of asking "Would you use this?", ask about their current behavior, problems, and constraints (TMT Ch.1).
- **Bad data is worse than no data**: If you ask leading questions, you get bad data. If you pitch your idea and they say "that's great," you got bad data (TMT Ch.1, Ch.2).
- **The only valid validation is commitment**: Someone says they'll pay, they sign a letter of intent, they give you a deposit. Compliments and interest are not validation (TMT Ch.2).
- **Define hypotheses, then test them**: A startup is an experiment. Define your leap-of-faith assumptions and design experiments to test them (LS Ch.5, Ch.7).
- **Build-Measure-Learn**: The fundamental feedback loop. Build a minimum product, measure customer response, learn whether to pivot or persevere (LS Ch.7).
- **MVP is about learning, not about product**: The Minimum Viable Product is the smallest thing you can build that starts the Build-Measure-Learn loop (LS Ch.6).
- **Validated learning is the unit of progress**: Progress is measured by what you learn about customers, not by lines of code shipped (LS Ch.3).
- **Don't fall in love with your solution, fall in love with the problem**: The solution will change as you learn. The problem is what matters (Inspired Ch.4, TMT Ch.1).
- **Customer segments and jobs-to-be-done**: Define who the customer is and what job they are trying to get done. Products get hired to do jobs (Inspired Ch.3, LS Ch.5).
- **A good strategy has a diagnosis, a guiding policy, and coherent actions**: Validation should test whether the diagnosis is correct (GSBS Ch.1).

## 6. Step-by-step method

### Step 1: Define the hypothesis
- Write down your fundamental assumptions (LS Ch.5):
  - *Value hypothesis*: Does the customer find value in what we're building?
  - *Growth hypothesis*: How will we reach new customers?
  - *Problem hypothesis*: Is this a real problem they are actively trying to solve?
  - *Solution hypothesis*: Will our proposed solution actually solve the problem?
- Define the customer segment: who specifically is the target customer? (Inspired Ch.3)
- Define the job-to-be-done: what job are they hiring your product to do? (Inspired Ch.3)
- Write each hypothesis as a testable statement: "We believe that [customer segment] has [problem] and will [behavior]."

### Step 2: Design customer conversations (not pitches)
- Do NOT pitch your idea. Do NOT ask "Would you use this?" (TMT Ch.1)
- Ask about their current behavior:
  - "How do you handle [problem] today?"
  - "What's the hardest part about [domain]?"
  - "When was the last time you tried to solve this?"
  - "Why did you stop / keep doing it?"
- Listen for specifics, not generics: specific stories are truth, generic opinions are lies (TMT Ch.2).
- Look for commitment signals: "Yes, I would pay for that" is cheap. "Can I sign up for a trial?" is stronger. "Here is my credit card" is real (TMT Ch.2).

### Step 3: Identify three types of bad data
- **Compliments**: "That's a great idea." Translate to "I'm being polite." (TMT Ch.1)
- **Fluff**: "I would definitely use that." Ask "What are you using today?" to verify. (TMT Ch.2)
- **Hypotheticals**: "I would pay $X for that." Ask them to actually pay. (TMT Ch.2)
- When you hear these, pivot the conversation: "What are you actually doing today? Can you show me?"

### Step 4: Run experiments
- For each hypothesis, design the simplest experiment that can falsify it (LS Ch.7):
  - *Smoke test*: A landing page with "Buy now" to test if anyone clicks.
  - *Concierge MVP*: Manually deliver the service to learn what's needed.
  - *Wizard of Oz*: Pretend the product works, deliver the output manually.
  - *Single-feature MVP*: Build just the core feature.
- Define the success criteria before running the experiment: what would make this hypothesis true or false? (LS Ch.7)

### Step 5: Measure and learn
- Collect data from the experiment. Use both quantitative (conversion rates, signups) and qualitative (customer interviews, feedback) (LS Ch.7).
- Compare results to the success criteria defined earlier
- Learn: what did we learn about the hypothesis? Did the customer actually take the commitment action?
- Decide: pivot (change the hypothesis) or persevere (continue with more investment) (LS Ch.8)

### Step 6: Iterate
- If the hypothesis was validated, refine it and test the next assumption
- If the hypothesis was invalidated, pivot: change the customer segment, problem, or solution
- Each cycle should be faster than the previous one (LS Ch.7)
- Keep a decision journal: what did we believe, what did we test, what did we learn, what did we decide?

## 7. Decision rules
- **If 3 out of 5 customer conversations produce compliments without specifics, you are not learning. Change the conversation format** (TMT Ch.1).
- **If no customer has taken a commitment action (money, time, referral) by conversation 10, the hypothesis is likely wrong** (TMT Ch.2).
- **If the MVP takes more than 2-3 weeks to build, it's too big. Find a smaller experiment** (LS Ch.6).
- **If the experiment results are inconclusive (neither clearly validates nor invalidates), tighten the success criteria** (LS Ch.7).
- **If the team has built features that no customer has asked for, you are in solution-land. Step back to problem-land** (Inspired Ch.4, TMT Ch.1).
- **If a customer says "I would pay for that" but can't describe their current solution (pen, paper, spreadsheet), they don't have a real problem** (TMT Ch.2).
- **If the diagnosis is wrong, the best strategy will fail. Validate the diagnosis before designing the solution** (GSBS Ch.1).

## 8. Common mistakes
- Falling in love with the solution and ignoring evidence that the problem doesn't exist (Inspired Ch.4, TMT Ch.1).
- Asking leading questions: "Would you use a service that does X?" (Everyone says yes) (TMT Ch.1).
- Pitching the idea instead of asking about the customer's life (TMT Ch.1).
- Building a full product instead of an MVP to test the riskiest assumption (LS Ch.6).
- Confusing customer interest with customer commitment (TMT Ch.2).
- Continuing to build after validation fails (ego, sunk cost) (LS Ch.8).
- Choosing vanity metrics (downloads, page views) over actionable metrics (retention, revenue) (LS Ch.7).
- Not defining success criteria before running the experiment (LS Ch.7).
- Interviewing the wrong customer segment (friends, family, people who love you) (TMT Ch.3).
- Assuming that a "yes" to a hypothetical question is data (TMT Ch.2).

## 10. Quality checklist
- [ ] Hypotheses are falsifiable and testable
- [ ] Customer segment is specific (not "everyone")
- [ ] Customer conversation guide avoids leading questions and pitches
- [ ] MVP is the smallest experiment that tests the riskiest assumption
- [ ] Success criteria are defined before the experiment
- [ ] Commitment signal is defined (not just "they liked it")
- [ ] Pivot/persevere criteria are defined
- [ ] Customer interviews are focused on past behavior, not future intentions
- [ ] Bad data signals are recognized and corrected
- [ ] Decision journal is maintained

## 11. Source books used
- Primary: The Mom Test (Fitzpatrick)
- Support: The Lean Startup (Ries), Inspired (Cagan), Good Strategy Bad Strategy (Rumelt)

## 12. Notes on how the books complement each other
TMT provides the critical skill of customer conversation: how to ask questions that produce real data, how to avoid bad data (compliments, fluff, hypotheticals), and how to spot commitment signals. LS provides the experimental framework: Build-Measure-Learn, MVP, validated learning, pivot/persevere. Inspired adds the product management perspective: customer segments, jobs-to-be-done, and the distinction between problem space and solution space. GSBS adds the strategy layer: diagnosis before policy, testing the diagnosis, and the importance of focusing on the critical challenge. Together they form a complete validation methodology from conversation to experiment to strategy.
